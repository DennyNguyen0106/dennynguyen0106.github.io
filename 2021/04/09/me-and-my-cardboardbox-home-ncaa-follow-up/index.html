Me and my cardboardbox home: NCAA follow-up

2021-04-09

So. I bet everything I owned on the NCAA tournament based on what my model told me and I am now homeless. The Colgate-Iowa final did not happen. But while I did lose everything, I learned some valuable lessons.

The most surprising thing about this whole process was that I was somehow not last. I know what you’re thinking, “you picked Colgate-Iowa for your final and you still didn’t get last? How can anyone lose to that?” which isn’t a bad thing to wonder. I have no idea myself, but the difference was 40 points in the Sweet 16, so that saved me.

In the first round, my model incorrectly guessed 10 games. The following games and their prediction percentage:

-Virginia beats Ohio (74%) -Connecticut beats Maryland (56%) -BYU beats UCLA (60%) -Texas beats Abil Christian (74%) -Colgate beats Arkansas (56%) -Ohio State beats Oral Roberts (83%) -Purdue beats North Texas (74%) -Texas Tech beats Utah State (60%) -Tennessee beats Oregon State (73%) -San Diego State beats Syracruse (60%)

So out of my peers, 6/17 people did better in predicting the first round than I did (just because I know what you’re wondering, I did better than Matt Waite by two games). Based on my model, five of the games were major upsets with prediction numbers going above 70%. The other five were closer to coin flips, ranging from 56-60%. Out of these games, the only one that I really disagreed with was Colgate-Arkansas, but again, if Colgate really did end up winning it all then I would be swarmed by many sports gambling businesses to throw money at me for my model.

But overall, looking at just my first round of games, it wasn’t anything too ergrigious. It became real bad when Colgate started to win anything. So the question is… why did my model like Colgate that much? It’s no question that Colgate did not play any real upper-tier competition, so I did include weighted wins into my model to try to balance it out. My theory is that total rebounds, while normally a really good predictor for wins, definitely was skewed because of the competition that Colgate played, so their numbers were extremely inflated. But another reason why I can’t say I was too upset with my model was because of the teams (not including Colgate) they had advancing into the Sweet 16. Coming into the tournament, I was a big fan of the Big Ten, and thought that the Sweet 16 would have five Big Ten teams. I do believe that if it was a best of three, many of the Big Ten teams would actually be in the Sweet 16, but that’s part of the madness in March Madness.

It’s hard to determine whether or not I could do better on my own compared to my model, because my model did predict Colgate to win it all. In my original model, the reason why I did a lot better was because I picked Gonzaga to win it, so that already gives me a lot more points. But as of right now, my model seemed to believe a lot of the same things as me, such as putting more faith into the Big Ten when we really shouldn’t. My model does need some fine tuning, but again, I don’t hate my model, I just hate the Colgate pick.

I will now start fixing my model and saving up money, so I can bet it all again next year. It will be wonderful when I get the first perfect bracket with the correct score in the final game as well.

Until then, back into the cardboard box for me.
